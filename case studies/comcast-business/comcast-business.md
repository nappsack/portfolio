## Project Overview

The ActiveCore project is a multi-year, ongoing, dedicated and embedded project with a mix of Think and Comcast designers. The design team consisted of a Comcast design manager and a rotation of 2-3 visual and UX designers.

### Overview of the Product

ActiveCore is a network engineering platform that allows enterprise Comcast Business customers to monitor and manage their software-defined wide access network (SDWAN). I arrived on the team just before its initial release, and worked alongside 2 other designers to push the product beyond its MVP phase.

### The Challenge

Take revolutionary SDWAN technology and put it into the hands of customers in a digestible, easy to understand way, without them having to be certified IT specialists. And at the same time, find ways to include comprehensive network configuration tools that would typically require an engineer.

### Key Objectives

- User friendly, simple, and easy to understand
- At the same time, allow for complex, advanced network configuration
- Accessible and available on all breakpoints, including a native mobile app for iOS and Android
- Scalable for both large and small networks

### Design Process

1. New feature comes in from product owner
2. Research and synthesis with users
3. Tech discovery to determine feasibility
4. UX exploration and wireframing
5. Visual design and prototype
6. Deliver to development

## UX / UI

### Native Mobile Experience

There is a separate experience for native iOS and Android phones, focused more on network monitoring and less on configuration. The user's mobile dashboard gives them a high-level overview of the health and performance of their networks and allows them to drill deeper into specific devices to monitor and troubleshoot anything that might need their attention.

### Some Cool Features I Designed

While I worked on dozens of incremental features and updates, below are some of the larger features that involved considerable amounts of research, concepting, and design iterations.

**Custom Site Groups**
This feature gave users the ability to create custom groups of sites / devices on their network, allowing them to apply more personalized filtering when viewing activity and analytics. I created this new pattern allowing users to select or remove sites between 2 buckets to create their groups.

**Top Talkers Analytics**
One of the most common requests was for the ability for business owners to see the users or applications that were consuming the most data on their network, giving business owners insights into how they can save money and energy. I designed a new series of charts with complex filtering mechanisms allowing the user to toggle between top users and top applications data.

**Custom JLL Thresholds**
Probably the largest and most painstaking feature I worked on, this gave users the ability to set custom performance metrics for each device on their network in order to more meticulously track their performance. Prior to this, they were only able to set network-level thresholds. I designed a Mad-Libs-style side panel that guided the user through a series of inputs in order to create this new notification.

## Design System

When I started on Comcast Business's product UX team, the design system (or lack thereof) was in shambles. It had evolved from a simple marketing style guide designed by an outside agency, and never accounted for product environments. 3 products were being built when I started, each with their own design team, with their designers just making it up as they went along. Another Think designer and I took it upon ourselves to completely rethink and rebuild their UI kit, processes, and governance of the system.

### Federated Model

After some research, we determined that a federated model would be the best option for our design system. This decision was made due to the significant level of complexity involved in the structure of Comcast Business's different design departments, which are split into 3 verticals: Marketing (learn, shop experience), Product UX, and Sales.

We started by focusing just on the product UX department, which is made up of multiple products, each with their own design team. Each product would elect 1 designer to be the design system ambassador for that product, who would attend weekly governance meetings and be the sole interfacer between their product and the design system. We broke the UI kit into 3 parts:

1. **Foundations** — Contains grid/spacing, type, colors, buttons, and iconography.
2. **Shared Patterns** — Contains larger patterns like form fields, controls, navigation, modals, etc.
3. **Product-specific Pattern Libraries** — Individual libraries for each product, consisting of product-specific patterns and page templates.

### Zeroheight Storefront

We created a virtual storefront for our design system using Zeroheight, where we were able to link all our assets directly to the storefront annotations and direct downloads. This became our one-stop-shop for all information regarding how design patterns should be used as well as their behaviors, states, and associated assets.

### Governance

We created a cadence of weekly UI kit governance meetings that brought ambassadors from every product together to discuss adding, removing, or modifying patterns. The process followed a decision flow: Does the component exist? Do changes, additions, or any new work need to happen? Does it fulfill requirements? Then the governance team would discuss how to introduce the new component.

## Tools & Processes

During my time with Comcast Business Product, I discovered my passion for revolutionary design tools and the structures and processes that come with them. I was fortunate enough that CB leadership was deeply motivated to invest in new tools to enhance our design process. I led the effort to adopt Abstract to improve collaboration and version control and eventually the migration to Figma, which consolidated 4 tools into one and totally transformed how we designed.

### Abstract

The move to Abstract was an important milestone in my design career. At this point, I had become an expert in Sketch and was very excited by the prospect of adding collaboration and version control. We were among the first adopters of Abstract in the industry and would meet regularly with advocates from the company to help figure out the best way to structure our project. It wasn't until the advocates at Abstract started asking us questions about how we were using the tool that I realized just how new Abstract was, even for Abstract!

I sat down with some fellow designers and together we created and implemented a comprehensive system and process for designing within Abstract. I created detailed documentation, sent it out to the entire department, and began 1:1 onboarding sessions for each designer. I quickly became the strongest expert and advocate for Abstract in the department, regularly meeting with team members to ensure smooth onboarding, and modifying our processes and structure to fit everyone's needs.

### Figma

By now, every designer understands the value of Figma and how it has completely revolutionized the UX world. I had always been aware of Figma and would regularly play around with it to see if it was ready for full-scale adoption. It wasn't until Summer 2020, with the release of component variants and auto-layout, that I realized this thing was legit. I convinced Comcast Business Product UX leadership to, once again, take a huge risk, and allow me and the other designer on ActiveCore at the time to pilot the tool.

We got the green light in November and began a 3-month pilot, moving all of our designs to the new tool. It was a painstaking process that involved much trial and error. Not only were we fully migrating all design assets to this new tool, but we were actively learning the tool at the same time.

We finally developed a proof-of-concept, and pitched to formally adopt in January 2021. From there, Figma exploded within the Comcast Business Product UX department. Similar to Abstract, I took the lead on developing a formal process for Comcast Business designers and began 1:1 trainings. We implemented a weekly working session called Figma Fridays, where anyone was invited to come and ask questions as CB adopted the tool. Eventually, the other teams at Comcast Business took notice and began to follow suit, followed eventually by the Xfinity teams.

### Figma Onboarding Guide

I created a comprehensive onboarding guide for Comcast Business designers covering:

- **Important Terms** — Teams, Projects, Branches, Variants, Collaboration
- **Join Relevant Teams** — Teams are created for each work stream, like products, focus areas, and the design system
- **Project Structure (Products)** — Structuring projects by process (Resources, Exploration, Visual Design, Grooming, Released)
- **Project Structure (Design System)** — Foundations Libraries, Shared Pattern Libraries, Native Libraries, Product Libraries
- **File Structure (Pages)** — Cover, Exploration, Visual Design/Prototype, Components
- **Start Your Design** — Creating new files, version history
- **Branching** — Creating branches, merging, review process
- **Content Handoff** — Developer delivery system

### Developer Handoff

The delivery process gave us a new opportunity to creatively diminish the barrier between design and dev. With great power comes great responsibility. And with Figma comes the responsibility to not only create a good experience for your end users, but for non-designers entering your design files as well. I developed a system for delivering design work that included a list of remaining design actions, feature overview, table of contents, and helpful resources like guides on how to use Figma and links to the prototype and requirements info.
