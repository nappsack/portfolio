## Project Overview

In 2020, Merck approached Think to design a new portal to allow their Payors (health care professionals and administrators or HCPs) to safely and securely access content containing important information related to their drugs. Up until now, this process was extremely complex and not secure. When a Payor needed information on a drug that Merck developed, they would have to reach out to a Merck representative, who would then set up a time to present the content to the Payor, and then follow-up by emailing that content to the Payor.

The fully Think-staffed team was about 6 months into design when I joined the project. I immediately jumped in and took the lead on migrating them to the newly established Merck design system (Vivid360) and to Figma as their primary design tool, while assisting in the UX design, legal review, and delivery of the portal.

## UX / UI

### Merck Formulary Resources Portal

The Payor Portal (officially named the Merck Formulary Resources Portal) is a multi-year, dedicated portal design project, fully staffed by Think. It consists of a design lead, 2 designers, a content strategist, a tech lead, and a project manager.

**Overview**
Payor Portal is a secure content request portal that allows health care providers (HCPs) to quickly browse, request, and obtain secure and private resources related to Merck's products. There is also an experience dedicated to their scientific and pipeline information that must live separately.

**The Challenge**
Find a way to allow HCPs to access product and scientific resources in a way that is secure, is easy to use, and adheres to incredibly strict legal limitations. Because this will be tediously reviewed by the FDA, the legal parameters create incredibly challenging hurdles that threaten the overall user experience.

**Key Objectives**

- The portal must be user-friendly, easy to browse and request resources from, and secure
- Users should be able to save resources they have been granted access to
- Include all safety and indication information anywhere there is branded content that makes any kind of statement about what the drug is expected to do
- Separate branded content from scientific information

### Specific Areas I Designed

While owning design system adherence and Figma migration, I also supported the design process, alongside another designer. We each took on entire sections of the portal, some of which are highlighted below.

**Library & Requests**
The backbone of the Payor Portal is the ability for HCPs to bookmark, access, and request resources. The library and requests pages needed to adhere to strict legal guidelines, including keeping all branded content separated by brand. Once a user lands on the library page for a specific brand, they can view their activity, saved resources, and track all requests for that brand.

**Dashboard**
The dashboard is the first page the user sees once they've completed their eligibility and registration and have logged on. Many months of working sessions, testing, and legal reviews went into curating this page to provide the optimal content to the Payor at the forefront of the portal. They are able to see their library, upcoming appointments, active requests, and contacts at a glance.

## Design System

Merck is made up of many digital products, all of which need to closely adhere to Merck's branding. Think Company was hired by Merck in 2019 to begin the process of auditing, cataloging, and streamlining all the components that make up their digital ecosystem into a scalable, usable design system, called Vivid360. Payor was the first product to be built after the creation of the V360 system. With my previous expertise in managing and implementing design systems at Comcast Business, Think decided to bring me onto the Payor team to help the team adopt and implement the Vivid360 team's work for the first time in a Merck product.

### Migrating to Figma

The Vivid360 design system was about 85% complete when I started on Payor, but it all lived in Sketch Cloud, since that was still the primary design tool being used by Merck. Thankfully, we got authorization to use Figma for the Payor Portal. I worked independently to migrate the entire Vivid360 design system into Figma, rebuilding each component to properly utilize Figma features like auto layout and component variants.

### Design System Adherence

As the visual designer and the most senior designer on the project, I was responsible first and foremost to make sure our designs adhered to the latest release of the Vivid360 design system. Once the UI kit was fully migrated into Figma, I worked with the other designer on the team to redesign all the existing pages so that they aligned to the Vivid360 system.

## Tools & Processes

As previously mentioned, Sketch was the primary design tool at Merck when I joined the Payor team; they had already created the initial designs and prototype using Sketch and Invision. I proposed migrating to Figma and was able to get approval from our stakeholders. This coincided with the Vivid360 adherence, becoming an opportunity to both redesign the portal with V360 components and migrate to Figma.

### Finding the Right *Project* Structure

Finding the perfect way to organize your files in Figma is a process. It's important to allow for seamless design work that can transition between phases and designers without missing a beat. I used projects on the Figma dashboard to separate our files by phases of design, enabling designers to easily move files between the projects as they moved through the design process from exploration to delivery.

### Finding the Right *File* Structure

The file structure proved to be another difficult challenge to tackle. I wanted the main design file to work as a Payor pattern library, with all pages and elements transformed into components that would be shareable throughout the rest of the Merck ecosystem. I settled on the concept of "base pages," where all the main design screens lived. By turning these into components, I was then able to place instances of those components into the prototype page, the legal review file, and eventually the delivery file, allowing us to keep all our main design assets in one single location.

### Object Oriented UX Workshop

Working with a brilliant content strategist is a game changer, especially on such a content-heavy project, but I realized early on that we weren't quite speaking the same language. With the content model living primarily in a spreadsheet and the designs living in Figma, we were both feeling intimidated whenever trying to decipher the other's work. We decided to run an OOUX workshop with our team and develop a visualized mapping of the content model. This allowed us to understand the content as objects in the user's journey, all of which have behaviors, metadata, attributes, CTAs, and nested objects.

### RRT Reviews

One of the major aspects of this project is the considerable amount of legal limitations. When working in a pharma environment, the FDA needs to see everything. So before it comes to that, Merck uses their rapid response teams (RRT) of legal professionals to meet with designers for weekly reviews to make sure there are no legal issues with the designs. This creates a very complex additional challenge. Not only are we trying to create the best user experience, but we are also responsible for complying with very strict legal restrictions, which most of the time are at the expense of the end user. These reviews can be tedious and difficult, and fairly often result in full-scale redesigns of entire pages in order to meet compliance.

### Developer Handoff

When it came time to deliver our work, I grabbed the same system I had developed over at Comcast Business, and improved upon it. Along with a comprehensive table of contents and resources, I provided an overview of the design team with profile cards, and added even more comprehensive annotations with a global components section.
